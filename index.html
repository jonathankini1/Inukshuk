What Is an Inukshuk?

An Inukshuk is a stone landmark built by the Inuit peoples of the Canadian Arctic. Traditionally used as navigation markers across vast, featureless landscapes, they are human-shaped figures assembled from carefully balanced stones — each one unique, none held together by mortar.

Today, they stand as symbols of guidance, hope, friendship, and the power of working together. Each Inukshuk is a communal act — the stones are too heavy for one person alone. It takes a gathering to raise one.

We bring this tradition to private estates across the UK and beyond. Your Inukshuk becomes a permanent landmark on your land — built with your people, from your stone.

From the Inuktitut: "in the likeness of a human"

Our Story

It started on a 13-acre property in Manchester. A family, their friends, and their neighbours decided to build something that would stand long after they were gone.

"The stones were too heavy for one person. That was the whole point."

With diggers, concrete, and a lot of determination, they raised an Inukshuk on their land. The day became something none of them expected — not a construction project, but a shared experience that drew people closer together.

That first build proved something: the act of building together creates something more powerful than the structure itself. Now we help others create that same experience on their own land.

Frequently Asked Questions

The practical details behind creating a permanent landmark on your land.

Where does the stone come from?

We prioritise stone that belongs visually and geologically to the setting. Where suitable material exists on your land, we assess it first; otherwise we source from an appropriate local quarry or reclaimed supply.

How long does a project take?

The on-site build is typically one to several days, depending on scale. The full programme also includes the site visit, design, engineering review, stone selection, logistics and ground preparation.

Will it need planning permission?

Requirements vary by height, location, land designation and local authority. We flag likely considerations during the site assessment, but formal planning advice should come from your local planning authority or appointed adviser.

How is the structure made safe?

Every build is designed around its scale, ground conditions and exposure. Larger commissions include an engineering review and a purpose-designed foundation before any stone is lifted into place.

Can family and friends take part?

Yes. Participation is central to the experience. The professional team manages machinery, lifting and safety while creating meaningful, supervised ways for your group to help raise the landmark.

Where do you work?

We work across the UK and will consider projects further afield. Travel, specialist transport and local logistics are scoped clearly before a commission begins.
